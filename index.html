About

Research-to-Practice
Engineer

I connect laboratory rigor to field reality — and field reality to the decisions that shape infrastructure networks.

I am a PhD Candidate in Civil Engineering at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, where I serve as a Graduate Research Assistant at the Illinois Center for Transportation. My doctoral research bridges flexible pavement material behavior and structural response to inform agency-level decision-making — integrating laboratory characterization, field evaluation, mechanistic modeling, and network-scale management.

My professional trajectory spans over a decade across research, field, and industry environments in Brazil, the United Kingdom, and the United States. I have led applied research programs for IDOT, worked as a Civil Engineer on Brazil's Transnordestina Railway, and held field and plant roles in asphalt construction and airport pavement QA/QC.

I believe technical rigor is not the opposite of innovation — it is its precondition. The most consequential improvements to engineering practice come from understanding, with depth and honesty, why current solutions perform the way they do and where they fall short.

IDOT · ICT R27-2672025–PresentStructural Evaluation · Network-Scale

Illinois Flexible Pavement Design & Monitoring

Advancing network-level pavement integrity studies by synthesizing structural performance data from over 20 years of extended-life (perpetual) pavement sections. Integrates FWD and Traffic Speed Deflectometer (TSD) measurements with laboratory analysis of field-extracted cores to evaluate the modulus-fatigue relationship and Remaining Service Life (RSL), accounting for evolving material technologies, environmental stressors, and modern mobility patterns.

Network-scale FWD and TSD data collection and backcalculation program
Lab fatigue and modulus characterization of cores from long-term pavement sections
RSL framework connecting material behavior to field structural performance trajectories
Deliverables directly informing IDOT extended-life pavement design guidelines

Pavement Friction Management Program — Illinois

Developing a statewide friction management framework for IDOT. Leads aggregate characterization via AIMS (morphology) and Micro-Deval (degradation) to identify priority sources and gradation bands for surface mixtures and chip seals. Outcomes feed a crash-risk threshold and resurfacing prioritization model, directly connecting pavement engineering to road safety policy.

AIMS-based aggregate morphology database for Illinois surface mix and chip seal sources

Personal

Beyond the Lab
& the Office

I am Brazilian. I have lived in the UK and the American Midwest. Moving between cultures is one of the best engineering educations I have had.

It teaches you to listen carefully, communicate across difference, and find common ground quickly — skills I consider just as critical in engineering as any technical method. Every project involves people with different priorities, backgrounds, and pressures. The ability to navigate that is not soft; it is structural.

My teaching experience reinforced this. Making complex mechanics intuitive to skeptical undergraduates trains a communicative patience that carries directly into project teams, client meetings, and field coordination. I am the kind of engineer who finds the solution with the team, not around it.

I thrive in dynamic, high-energy environments. Big cities energize me — the density, the complexity, the pace. I am fully open to relocating, and drawn to the problems that only dense urban infrastructure creates: constrained sites, competing stakeholder interests, legacy systems, and solutions that must be simultaneously rigorous and deployable.

Outside of work, I run long distances — which has taught me more about pacing, endurance, and honest self-assessment than almost anything else. I explore cities on foot, which turns out to be an excellent way to develop opinions about pavement quality.
